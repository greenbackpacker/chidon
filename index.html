Torah Bereshit בְּרֵאשִׁית · Genesis
▾
Genesis 4 Cain & Abel Aftermath / Seth's Line
Aftermath of the First Murder & the Line of Seth
After killing Abel, Cain is cursed by God to be a "restless wanderer" on the earth. Cain protests that the punishment is too great — anyone who finds him will kill him. God places a protective mark on Cain so that no one will slay him. Cain settles in the land of Nod ("wandering"), east of Eden, and builds a city named after his son Enoch. His descendant Lamech boasts of killing a man for wounding him — foreshadowing the violence that corrupts humanity. Meanwhile, Adam and Eve have another son: Seth ("appointed"), who is born to replace Abel. Seth has a son named Enosh. The chapter ends noting that it was during Enosh's time that people began to call upon the name of God (יְהוָה).
Key Details to Remember
  • Cain's mark = protection, not punishment
  • Cain's city = named "Enoch" after his son
  • Lamech = first bigamist; boasts of killing a young man
  • Seth = replaces Abel; his son is Enosh
  • Calling on God's name begins in Enosh's generation
Genesis 6:1–22 Corruption / God's Warning to Noah
Humanity's Corruption & God Chooses Noah
The "sons of God" (b'nei ha-Elohim) take human women as wives — a mysterious passage often debated. God sees that human wickedness is great and that "every inclination of the human heart was only evil all the time." God regrets creating humanity and decides to blot out all life. But Noah "found favor (חֵן) in God's eyes" — he is described as righteous (צַדִּיק) and blameless (תָּמִים) in his generation and "walked with God." God tells Noah the earth is filled with חָמָס (violence/lawlessness). God instructs Noah to build an ark (תֵּיבָה) of cypress wood, coated inside and out with pitch, with specific dimensions: 300 cubits long, 50 wide, 30 high. It must have a roof, a door, and three decks. God promises to establish a covenant (בְּרִית) with Noah. Noah is to bring his wife, his three sons, their wives, and two of every living creature onto the ark, plus food. Noah did everything exactly as God commanded.
Key Details to Remember
  • Noah = righteous + blameless + walked with God
  • Ark dimensions: 300 × 50 × 30 cubits; 3 decks
  • Material: cypress wood (gofer), coated with pitch (כֹּפֶר)
  • God's reason for flood: חָמָס — violence/lawlessness
  • "Noah did all that God commanded him" (exact obedience)
Genesis 7:1–24 The Flood
The Flood Begins
God tells Noah to enter the ark with his household. He is to take seven pairs (14) of every clean animal and one pair of every unclean animal. Rain will fall for 40 days and 40 nights. Noah is 600 years old when the flood begins. All board the ark: Noah, his wife, his three sons (Shem, Ham, Japheth), and their wives. The "windows of heaven" open and the "fountains of the great deep" burst — this is cosmic imagery of the waters above and below the earth returning to primordial chaos. The waters rise until they cover the highest mountains by 15 cubits. All living creatures on dry land die — every person, animal, bird, and creeping thing. Only Noah and those with him in the ark survive. The flood waters prevail for 150 days.
Key Details to Remember
  • Clean animals: 7 pairs; unclean: 1 pair
  • Rain: 40 days and 40 nights
  • Noah's age at flood: 600 years old
  • Waters rose 15 cubits above the tallest mountains
  • Flood lasted 150 days total
  • Noah's sons: Shem, Ham, Japheth
Genesis 8:1–22 Waters Recede
The Flood Recedes & Noah Leaves the Ark
God "remembered" Noah — the turning point. God sends a wind over the earth. The fountains and windows close. After 150 days, the ark rests on Mount Ararat. Mountains appear on the first day of the 10th month. After 40 more days, Noah opens a window and sends out a raven (עֹרֵב), which flies back and forth. Then he sends out a dove (יוֹנָה), which returns because there's no dry land. He waits 7 days and sends the dove again — it returns with an olive leaf (עֲלֵה זַיִת) in its mouth. He waits another 7 days; the dove flies out and does not return. Noah removes the covering of the ark and sees the ground is dry. God tells him to come out with all the animals. Noah builds an altar (מִזְבֵּחַ) and offers burnt offerings. God smells the "pleasing aroma" and resolves: "Never again will I curse the ground because of humans… nor will I ever destroy all living creatures as I have done." God establishes the regular seasons as a promise.
Key Details to Remember
  • Ark rests on Mount Ararat
  • First bird sent: raven; Second: dove
  • Olive leaf = sign of dry land returning
  • Noah builds an altar — first act outside the ark
  • God promises never again to destroy all life
  • Regular seasons (seed-time/harvest, summer/winter) guaranteed
Genesis 9:1–19 Covenant, Rainbow, Noah's Sons
The Noahide Covenant & The Rainbow
God blesses Noah and his sons with the same command as Adam: "Be fruitful and multiply." Now animals will fear humans. God permits eating meat, but not blood (נֶפֶשׁ) — blood is the life force. God establishes the principle: "Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed, for in the image of God was humanity made." God establishes His covenant (בְּרִית) with Noah, his descendants, and every living creature — the broadest covenant in the Torah. The sign of this covenant is a rainbow (קֶשֶׁת) in the clouds. God says: when I see the rainbow, I will remember My covenant and the waters will never again destroy all flesh. The three sons of Noah are named: Shem, Ham, and Japheth — from them the entire earth was populated.
Key Details to Remember
  • Permission to eat meat — but NOT blood
  • Murder = capital offense (image of God = sanctity of life)
  • Covenant includes humans AND every living creature
  • Rainbow = sign of the covenant
  • God's promise: never again flood to destroy all life
  • Shem, Ham, Japheth = ancestors of all nations
Genesis 11:1–9 Tower of Babel
The Tower of Babel
All humanity speaks one language (שָׂפָה אֶחָת). They migrate eastward and settle in the land of Shinar (שִׁנְעָר). They decide to build a city and a tower reaching the heavens, saying: "Let us make a name for ourselves (נַעֲשֶׂה לָּנוּ שֵׁם)." God "comes down" to see the city — ironic, since their great tower barely reaches God. God says: if they can do this as one people with one language, nothing will be beyond them. So God scatters them (הֵפִיץ) across the earth and confuses (בָּלַל) their language — this is the origin of the word Babel (בָּבֶל). The building stops. This chapter explains the diversity of languages and nations.
Key Details to Remember
  • Location: land of Shinar (=Babylonia/Mesopotamia)
  • Building material: bricks (not stone) and tar (not mortar)
  • Human goal: "make a name for ourselves"
  • God's response: scatter and confuse language
  • Babel (בָּבֶל) = "confused" — wordplay on Babylon
Genesis 35:1–29 Return to Beit El; Deaths of Rachel & Yitzchak
Return to Beit El & Family Deaths
God tells Jacob to go to Beit El (בֵּית אֵל) and build an altar — where God appeared to him when he fled Esau. Jacob tells his household to put away their foreign gods, purify themselves, and change their garments. He buries all the idols and earrings under the oak at Shechem. They travel to Beit El, and God appears again, reaffirming the name change from Jacob to Israel and repeating the promises of land and descendants. God also calls him El Shaddai (אֵל שַׁדַּי). Jacob sets up a pillar (מַצֵּבָה) and pours oil on it. On the road from Beit El, Rachel goes into labor with Benjamin (בִּנְיָמִין) — her dying wish is to name him Ben-Oni ("son of my sorrow"), but Jacob calls him Benjamin ("son of the right hand/south"). Rachel dies and is buried on the road to Ephrath (Bethlehem); Jacob sets a pillar over her grave. Then Reuben lies with Bilhah (his father's concubine). The chapter closes with a list of Jacob's 12 sons and the death of Isaac (Yitzchak) at age 180, buried by Esau and Jacob.
Key Details to Remember
  • Jacob buries foreign gods at Shechem before going to Beit El
  • God renames Jacob "Israel" again here (also happened at Penuel)
  • Rachel's dying name for Benjamin: Ben-Oni; Jacob changes it
  • Rachel buried near Bethlehem (Ephrath) — NOT in the Cave of Machpelah
  • Reuben's sin with Bilhah — noted but not punished yet
  • Isaac dies at age 180; buried by both Esau and Jacob
Torah Devarim דְּבָרִים · Deuteronomy
▾
Deuteronomy 1:1–33 Learning from Our Past
Moses' Retelling: The Journey & the Spies
Moses begins his farewell speech to the Israelites on the plains of Moab. He recounts how God commanded them to leave Horeb (Sinai) and go to Canaan. Moses recalls appointing leaders (שָׂרִים) and judges because the people were too numerous for him to lead alone — advice he received from his father-in-law Jethro (implied). He recounts how they traveled through the wilderness. Moses describes reaching Kadesh-barnea and sending the 12 spies. The spies brought back good fruit but reported that the cities were fortified and the people were giants (Anakim). The people feared and refused to go in. God had promised: "Do not be terrified or afraid of them — the LORD your God goes before you" (verse 29). Yet the people did not trust God, who had carried them "as a father carries his son" through the wilderness.
Key Details to Remember
  • Moses is speaking on the plains of Moab — this is all one long speech
  • He appointed judges "wise, understanding, and experienced"
  • The spies left from Kadesh-barnea
  • The Anakim = giants the people feared
  • God compared to a father carrying his child through the desert
  • Failing to trust God = the core sin reviewed here
Deuteronomy 8:1–18 Gratitude / Birkat Hamazon
Gratitude for the Land & Source of Birkat HaMazon
Moses warns the people: remember the 40 years in the desert. God humbled them, let them hunger, and fed them manna (מָן) — to teach that humans do not live by bread alone but by every word from God's mouth. Their clothes did not wear out; their feet did not swell. God disciplines like a father. Now they are about to enter a good land with water, grain, vines, figs, pomegranates, olive oil, honey, iron, and copper. Verse 10 is the biblical source for Birkat HaMazon: "And you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the LORD your God for the good land He gave you." Moses' great warning: Do not forget God when you prosper. Do not say in your heart, "My own power and strength made this wealth." Remember: it is God who gives you the power to create wealth.
Key Details to Remember
  • Verse 3: "not by bread alone" — humans live by God's word
  • Manna was a test of humility and dependence
  • Verse 10 = source for Birkat HaMazon (grace after meals)
  • The "good land" described in lush detail (7 species implied)
  • Key warning: prosperity leads to forgetting God
  • "My power made this wealth" = the danger Moses warns against
Deuteronomy 10:12–22 Caring for the Stranger
What God Asks of You & Love for the Stranger
Moses gives the essence of what God requires: "And now, Israel, what does the LORD your God ask of you but to fear God, walk in His ways, love Him, serve Him with all your heart and soul, and observe God's commandments." God does not need anything — the universe belongs to God. Yet God chose the patriarchs and their descendants in love. Moses commands: circumcise the foreskin of your heart (מוּל אֶת עָרְלַת לְבַבְכֶם) — be spiritually open, not stiff-necked. God is the God of gods and Lord of lords, who does not show favoritism or take bribes, who executes justice for the orphan and widow and loves the stranger (גֵּר), giving him food and clothing. "You shall love the stranger, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt."
Key Details to Remember
  • Famous verse: fear, walk, love, serve, observe — the five-fold demand
  • "Circumcise your heart" = metaphor for spiritual openness
  • God of gods, Lord of lords — unique among ancient Near Eastern theology
  • God protects orphan, widow, and stranger
  • "Love the stranger — for you were strangers in Egypt"
Deuteronomy 11:13–21 2nd Paragraph of Shema
VeHayah Im Shamoa — Second Paragraph of the Shema
This passage is the second paragraph of the Shema, recited twice daily. It sets up a conditional covenant: IF you love and serve God with all your heart and soul, THEN God will provide rain in its season — the early rain (יוֹרֶה) and late rain (מַלְקוֹשׁ) — and grain, wine, and oil. Your cattle will have grass. You will eat and be satisfied. BUT if you turn away to worship other gods, God's anger will burn and the sky will withhold rain. Therefore: write these words on your hearts, teach them to your children, speak of them at home and away, at bedtime and rising. Bind them as a sign on your hand and as frontlets between your eyes (tefillin). Write them on the doorposts of your house and gates (mezuzah).
Key Details to Remember
  • This IS the second paragraph of the Shema (recited daily)
  • Conditional structure: obey = rain; disobey = drought
  • Early rain (yoreh) + late rain (malkosh) = agricultural seasons
  • Commands: teach children, bind on hand/forehead, write on doorposts
  • These commands are the origins of tefillin and mezuzah
Deuteronomy 26:1–11 Bikkurim
First Fruits — Bikkurim Declaration
When Israel enters the land and settles it, each person is to bring the first fruits (בִּכּוּרִים) of the soil to the central sanctuary. The priest will place the basket before the altar. The person then recites a famous declaration — called the "Arami Oved Avi" (אֲרַמִּי אֹבֵד אָבִי) passage: "My father was a wandering Aramean. He went down to Egypt with few people and sojourned there… the Egyptians oppressed us… we cried out to God… God brought us out with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm, with great awe, signs, and wonders, and brought us to this place and gave us this land flowing with milk and honey." The declaration concludes with presenting the basket to God and bowing. This passage is also the core of the Passover Haggadah's storytelling framework.
Key Details to Remember
  • Bikkurim = first fruits brought to the Temple (Shavuot connection)
  • "Arami Oved Avi" = the declaration recited with the basket
  • Covers: Aramean ancestor → Egypt → slavery → cry out → Exodus → Promised Land
  • This passage is the core script of the Passover Haggadah
  • Ends: rejoice with the Levite and stranger among you
Deuteronomy 30:11–20 Choose Life
The Torah Is Not Far Away — Choose Life!
Moses declares that the commandment is not too hard, not far away. It is not in heaven (you don't need to send someone to bring it down); it is not across the sea. "The word is very near you — in your mouth and in your heart to do it." Moses sets before Israel life and good vs. death and evil. Choose life means: love God, obey His voice, cling to Him — for God is your life and the length of your days, to dwell in the land. Moses then presents the ultimate choice: "I have set before you today life and death, blessing and curse — therefore choose life (וּבָחַרְתָּ בַּחַיִּים)" — so that you and your children may live.
Key Details to Remember
  • "The Torah is not in heaven" = accessible to everyone
  • The word is "in your mouth and heart to do it"
  • Binary choice: life/good vs. death/evil
  • Famous phrase: וּבָחַרְתָּ בַּחַיִּים — "choose life"
  • Choosing life = loving God + obeying + clinging to God

Exodus 30:11–16 Shabbat Shekalim · Before Adar
The Half-Shekel (מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל)
When Moses takes a census of Israel, every man 20 years and older must give a half-shekel (מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל) to God as an atonement offering. The rich shall not give more and the poor shall not give less — everyone equal before God. The money is used for the service of the Tent of Meeting and serves as a reminder (זִכָּרוֹן) before God to atone for the Israelites.
When & Why It's Read

Read on the Shabbat immediately before (or on) Rosh Chodesh Adar — about a month before Purim. This reminds people of the half-shekel tax due for Temple upkeep before Nisan. Also thematically connected to Haman's attempt to pay shekels to destroy Israel (Esther 3:9).

Key Details
  • Every male 20+ must contribute (army-age census)
  • Amount: half a shekel (of the sanctuary standard)
  • Rich = poor; everyone gives the same
  • Purpose: atonement + support of the Mishkan
Deuteronomy 25:17–19 Shabbat Zachor · Shabbat Before Purim
Remember Amalek! (זָכוֹר)
Israel is commanded to remember (זָכוֹר) what Amalek did — how they attacked the weak and weary stragglers at the back of the Israelite camp when they came out of Egypt. When God gives Israel rest from all their enemies, they must blot out (מָחָה) the memory of Amalek. The passage ends with the paradox: "Do not forget (לֹא תִשְׁכָּח)" — you must remember to blot out memory.
When & Why It's Read

Read on the Shabbat before Purim because Haman is identified as a descendant of Agag, king of Amalek. Reading this passage primes us for the Purim story as the ongoing battle against Amalek.

Key Details
  • Amalek attacked the weak/weary at the REAR of the camp
  • Amalek had "no fear of God" — key character note
  • Command: blot out Amalek's memory
  • Paradox: "remember" AND "do not forget" — both in same passage
  • Haman = Agagite (descendant of Amalek's king)
Numbers 19:1–22 Shabbat Parah · After Purim
The Red Heifer (פָּרָה אֲדֻמָּה)
God commands a completely red cow (פָּרָה אֲדֻמָּה) — without defect, never yoked — to be brought to Eleazar the priest. It is slaughtered outside the camp and burned with cedar wood, hyssop (אֵזוֹב), and crimson thread. The ashes are gathered and kept as "waters of lustration" (מֵי נִדָּה) — purification waters. Anyone who has become ritually impure through contact with a corpse is sprinkled with these waters on the third and seventh day to become pure. Paradoxically: the priest who performs the purification becomes impure, while the impure person becomes pure. This is considered the ultimate חֹק — a divine decree without rational explanation.
When & Why It's Read

Read after Purim to prepare people for Passover. Anyone who had become impure through contact with a corpse needed to use the red heifer waters in time to become pure before the Passover offering. The reading is a reminder to prepare spiritually and ritually.

Key Details
  • Red cow must be entirely red, without defect, never yoked
  • Burned with cedar wood, hyssop, and crimson thread
  • Ashes mixed with water = purification from corpse impurity
  • Great paradox: purifier becomes impure; impure person becomes pure
  • Classic example of a חֹק (unexplained divine law)
  • Sprinkled on days 3 and 7 of impurity
Exodus 12:1–20 Shabbat HaHodesh · Before Nisan
Sanctification of the New Month & Passover Laws
God tells Moses and Aaron in Egypt: "This month (Nisan) shall be for you the first of the months of the year." On the 10th of Nisan, each household must take a lamb (שֶׂה) — one per household (or share with a neighbor if the household is too small). The lamb must be a one-year-old male without blemish, taken from the sheep or goats. On the 14th of Nisan at twilight (בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם), the community slaughters it. The blood is smeared on the two doorposts and the lintel of each house. The lamb is eaten roasted — not raw, not boiled — with matzah and bitter herbs (מְרֹרִים). Nothing may be left until morning; anything left must be burned. You must eat it with your loins girded, sandals on, staff in hand — in haste. God will pass through Egypt and strike down the firstborn; the blood will be a sign and God will pass over (פָּסַח) those houses. Eat matzah for seven days; on the first day remove all leaven (חָמֵץ) from your houses. No leavened bread for 7 days.
When & Why It's Read

Read on the Shabbat before Rosh Chodesh Nisan (or on it) to announce the month of Passover and remind the people to prepare by removing chametz and preparing the Passover seder.

Key Details
  • Nisan = first month of the year (for calendar purposes)
  • Lamb selected on 10th; slaughtered on 14th of Nisan
  • Lamb: one year old, male, without blemish
  • Blood on doorposts + lintel (not threshold)
  • Eaten with matzah and bitter herbs; roasted (not boiled)
  • Eaten in haste: loins girded, sandals on, staff in hand
  • Seven days of matzah; chametz forbidden and removed
Three Weeks תְּלַת דְּפֻרְעָנוּתָא — Haftarot of Retribution
▾
Jeremiah 1:1–2:3 1st Shabbat after 17 Tammuz
Jeremiah's Call & God's Warning
God calls Jeremiah from before birth: "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you; before you were born I sanctified you." Jeremiah protests he is too young to speak. God touches his mouth — "I have placed My words in your mouth." God shows Jeremiah two visions: an almond branch (שָׁקֵד) — because God is "watching (שֹׁקֵד)" over His word to fulfill it. Then a boiling pot (סִיר נָפוּחַ) tipped from the north — disaster will come from the north against all the inhabitants of the land (Babylonia). God commissions Jeremiah to speak to all of Judah. In 2:1–3, God recalls Israel's devotion as a bride in the desert — a brief tender memory before the accusations to come.
When & Why

First of the Three Haftarot of Retribution, read the Shabbat after the 17th of Tammuz (the fast marking when the walls of Jerusalem were breached). The prophecy of coming destruction fits the season of mourning leading to Tisha B'Av.

Key Details
  • Two visions: almond branch (God is "watching") + boiling pot from north
  • Jeremiah called before birth; protests he's too young
  • God touches his mouth to give him words
  • 2:2–3: Israel described as a devoted bride in the desert
Jeremiah 2:4–28, 4:1–2 2nd Shabbat of the Three Weeks
Israel's Unfaithfulness — Accusations
God accuses Israel: What wrong did your ancestors find in Me that they went after worthless idols? God brought them through the wilderness, a land of deserts and darkness, and gave them a fruitful land — yet they defiled it and made it an abomination. The priests, rulers, and prophets all went after Baal. God challenges: has any nation traded away its gods? Yet Israel traded its Glory (God) for what does not profit. A horrifying image: Israel has committed two evils — they abandoned Me, the Fountain of Living Waters, and dug broken cisterns that hold no water. Chapter 4:1–2: If Israel will return, remove its detestable idols; then nations will bless themselves by God through Israel.
Key Details
  • Famous image: God = "Fountain of Living Waters" vs. broken cisterns
  • Nations don't abandon their gods — but Israel abandoned God
  • Accusation covers priests, rulers, and prophets
  • 4:1–2 holds out hope: return to God and be a blessing to nations
Isaiah 1:1–27 Shabbat Chazon · 3rd Shabbat / Before Tisha B'Av
The Vision of Isaiah — Rebuke Before Tisha B'Av
The opening of Isaiah is called "Chazon" (חֲזוֹן — vision). God says: "I raised children and they rebelled against me. The ox knows its master, the donkey its owner's manger — but Israel does not know, My people do not understand." The nation is sick from head to foot; the land is desolate. God is fed up with sacrifices and festivals if they are not accompanied by justice. "When you spread your hands in prayer, I will hide My eyes from you… Your hands are full of blood." God calls: "Wash yourselves, make yourselves clean… cease to do evil, learn to do good, seek justice, aid the oppressed, uphold the orphan's cause, defend the widow." Then comes hope: "Though your sins be like scarlet, they shall be white as snow." Jerusalem once was a faithful city — now a harlot. God will restore her: the city will again be called "City of Righteousness (עִיר הַצֶּדֶק), Faithful City (קִרְיָה נֶאֱמָנָה)".
When & Why

Shabbat Chazon is the Shabbat immediately before Tisha B'Av. The entire haftarah is one of rebuke and warning, fitting the most mournful day of the Jewish year.

Key Details
  • "Chazon" = name of this Shabbat (from the opening word of Isaiah)
  • Ox knows its master — even animals more faithful than Israel
  • God rejects sacrifices + festivals done without justice
  • "Though your sins be scarlet, they shall be white as snow"
  • Social justice demands: orphan, widow, oppressed
  • Jerusalem will be restored as a righteous, faithful city
7 Weeks of Comfort שִׁבְעָה דְּנֶחֱמָתָא — After Tisha B'Av
▾
Isaiah 40:1–26 Shabbat Nachamu · 1st Week
Comfort, Comfort My People (נַחֲמוּ עַמִּי)
The famous opening: "Nachamu nachamu ami — Comfort, comfort My people," says your God. Speak to Jerusalem's heart; her term of service is over, her sin has been paid for." A voice calls: "In the wilderness prepare the way of God; make straight a highway in the desert." All flesh is like grass — it withers. "But the word of our God stands forever." God comes with might but also "like a shepherd who tends his flock, gathers the lambs in his arms, carries them close to his heart." God measured the waters in the hollow of His hand; weighed the mountains in scales. Who can compare to God? The nations are a drop in a bucket. God does not grow weary or tired. Those who trust in God will renew their strength, "they will soar on wings like eagles."
When & Why

Shabbat Nachamu = the Shabbat immediately after Tisha B'Av. "Nachamu" is the first word of this haftarah. This is the beginning of the seven weeks of comfort that take us from Tisha B'Av to Rosh Hashanah.

Key Details
  • "Nachamu nachamu ami" — first words = name of this Shabbat
  • Jerusalem's "term of service" has ended; punishment paid
  • God as shepherd carrying lambs — tenderness after punishment
  • All flesh is grass; God's word stands forever
  • Nations = drop in a bucket compared to God
  • "Soar on wings like eagles" — those who trust God
Isaiah 49:14–51:3 2nd Week of Comfort
Zion Says She's Been Forgotten
Zion complains: "God has forsaken me, God has forgotten me." God responds with the most tender comfort: "Can a woman forget her nursing infant, have no compassion on the child of her womb? Even if she could forget, I will not forget you. I have engraved you on the palms of My hands." Your destroyers will leave; your children will return. The chapter assures Israel that captors will be taken from her and her children restored. Chapter 51 returns to Abraham: "Look to the rock from which you were cut — look to Abraham your father and Sarah who bore you." God will comfort Zion; "her wilderness will be like Eden, her desert like the Garden of God."
Key Details
  • "Can a mother forget her nursing child?" — most famous verse
  • "I have engraved you on the palms of My hands"
  • Zion's children will return; oppressors will leave
  • "Look to Abraham and Sarah" — call to remember the origins
  • Zion's wilderness will become like Eden
Isaiah 51:12–52:12 3rd Week of Comfort
Awake! — God Redeems Jerusalem
"I, I am He who comforts you — why do you fear mortals?" God reminds Zion: you have suffered the cup of staggering (God's fury) but now God takes it from your hand and gives it to your oppressors. "Awake, awake, put on your strength, O Zion! Put on your beautiful garments, O Jerusalem!" Shake off the dust; arise. How beautiful upon the mountains are "the feet of the messenger who announces peace… who says to Zion, 'Your God reigns!'" The watchmen will sing together as God returns to Zion. Go out from Babylon! "Depart, depart, go out from there… but you shall not go out in haste or in flight, for God goes before you, and the God of Israel is your rear guard."
Key Details
  • "Awake, awake, put on your strength O Zion!" — famous call
  • "How beautiful are the feet of the messenger of peace"
  • "Your God reigns!" — announced to Zion
  • Leave Babylon — but NOT in haste (contrast to Egypt)
  • God as vanguard AND rear guard of the departure
Isaiah 54:11–55:5 4th Week of Comfort
The Rebuilt Jerusalem — Covenant of Peace
God promises to rebuild Jerusalem with sapphires, rubies, and precious stones. "All your children shall be disciples of God, and your children's peace will be great." No weapon formed against Israel will succeed. Then 55:1: "Ho! All who are thirsty, come to the water! Even one who has no money — come, buy, and eat!" God offers an everlasting covenant — the enduring love promised to David — extended now to the whole nation. Nations that did not know Israel will run to her because of God's holiness.
Key Details
  • Jerusalem rebuilt with precious gems — sapphires, rubies
  • "All your children shall be taught by God"
  • "No weapon formed against you will succeed"
  • 55:1 — "Ho, all who are thirsty" — free invitation to God's abundance
  • Everlasting covenant = the "sure mercies of David" extended to all
Isaiah 54:1–10 5th Week of Comfort
The Barren One Will Sing — Covenant Restored
"Sing, O barren one who did not bear! Break forth into singing, you who did not labor in birth!" Zion, though barren and forsaken, will have more children than the married woman. Enlarge your tent! Spread wide! God admits to a brief abandonment: "For a brief moment I forsook you, but with great compassion I will gather you. In a surge of anger I hid My face from you for a moment, but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you." As the waters of Noah — God swore never to flood again. So God swears: "My kindness (חֶסֶד) shall not depart from you, and My covenant of peace (בְּרִית שָׁלוֹם) shall not be removed."
Key Details
  • "Sing O barren one" — Zion as a once-barren woman
  • God admits to "brief" abandonment — but eternal compassion
  • Comparison to Noah's flood: God swore that waters would never return
  • "Covenant of peace" (Brit Shalom) will not be removed
  • Key word: חֶסֶד (chesed) — everlasting kindness
Isaiah 60:1–22 6th Week of Comfort
Arise, Shine! — The Glory of Zion
"Arise, shine (קוּמִי אוֹרִי), for your light has come! The glory of God has risen upon you." Though darkness covers the earth, God's glory shines on Israel and nations will come to her light. Caravans of camels will bring gold and frankincense; the ships of Tarshish will bring children. "Foreigners will rebuild your walls; their kings will serve you." The gates of the city will always stand open. The sun and moon will no longer be needed — "God will be your everlasting light." The smallest will become a mighty nation; "I the LORD will hasten it in its time."
Key Details
  • "Arise, shine — your light has come" (first words, very famous)
  • Nations and kings come to Zion's light
  • Caravans of camels bringing gold and frankincense
  • No more need for sun or moon — God = everlasting light
  • Foreigners will rebuild Jerusalem's walls
Isaiah 61:10–63:9 7th Week of Comfort
Dressed in Salvation — God's Great Act of Redemption
"I will greatly rejoice in God… for He has dressed me in garments of salvation (בִּגְדֵי יֶשַׁע), wrapped me in a robe of righteousness, like a bridegroom adorning himself with a crown, like a bride adorned with her jewels." Righteousness and praise will spring up before all nations. Jerusalem will be called by a new name: no more "Forsaken (עֲזוּבָה)" or "Desolate (שְׁמָמָה)" but "My Delight Is In Her (חֶפְצִי בָהּ)" and "Married (בְּעוּלָה)". God has set watchmen on Jerusalem's walls. In 63, God is described as a warrior coming from Edom in blood-stained garments — having taken vengeance on Israel's enemies. "In all their afflictions God was afflicted… In His love and pity He redeemed them."
Key Details
  • "Garments of salvation" + "robe of righteousness" — beautiful imagery
  • Jerusalem's new names: "Hephzibah" (My delight) and "Beulah" (Married)
  • God as warrior from Edom — taking vengeance on enemies
  • "In all their afflictions, God was afflicted" — key theological idea
  • In love and pity He redeemed them throughout history

Hosea 14:2–10 Shabbat Shuvah · Between RH and YK
Return, O Israel (שׁוּבָה יִשְׂרָאֵל)
"Shuvah Yisrael — Return, O Israel, to the LORD your God, for you have stumbled in your iniquity." Take words with you and return. Say: "Forgive all iniquity, accept what is good; we will pay our lips as offerings of bulls." Assyria will not save us; we will not ride horses; we will no longer say 'our gods' to what our hands made. God promises: "I will heal their apostasy, I will love them freely… I will be like dew to Israel — he will blossom like the lily." They will dwell in God's shade, revive like grain, blossom like the vine. The chapter ends: "Who is wise? Let them understand these things… the ways of God are straight; the righteous walk in them, but transgressors stumble."
When & Why

Read on Shabbat Shuvah — the Shabbat between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. "Shuvah" = repentance/return. Perfect for the Ten Days of Repentance.

Key Details
  • "Shuvah Yisrael" = name of this Shabbat (from opening words)
  • Instruction to "take words" — prayer replaces animal sacrifice
  • God = dew; Israel blossoms like lily, vine, cedar of Lebanon
  • Ends with wisdom teaching: wise will understand God's ways
Isaiah 57:14–58:14 Yom Kippur Morning
True Fasting — Social Justice Over Ritual
God says: "Build up, build up the road! Clear the way!" God dwells in the high and holy place and also with the lowly and humble of spirit. Chapter 58: the people ask why God ignores their fasting. God responds: your fast is invalid — while you fast, you pursue your business, oppress your workers, quarrel and fight. "Is this the fast I choose — to bow your head like a reed, to lie in sackcloth and ashes?" The true fast: "Release the bonds of wickedness, undo the yoke of oppression, let the oppressed go free, share your bread with the hungry, bring the homeless poor into your home, clothe the naked." Then your light will break forth like dawn, your healing will quickly appear. "If you call the Shabbat a delight and honor it… then you will find joy in God."
When & Why

Read on Yom Kippur morning as the central message of the day: the fast alone is meaningless without genuine moral transformation and care for others.

Key Details
  • God is both transcendent AND with the lowly and humble
  • Invalid fast: oppress workers, argue, bow head in sackcloth only
  • True fast: release oppressed, feed hungry, clothe naked, house homeless
  • "Your light will break forth like dawn" — reward for true fasting
  • Shabbat as delight — also discussed in this haftarah
Judges 4:4–5:31 Parashat Beshalach
Deborah, Barak & the Song of Deborah
Deborah (דְּבוֹרָה), a prophetess, is judging Israel under a palm tree. She summons Barak son of Abinoam and commands him to fight Sisera, commander of Canaan's army. Barak refuses to go unless Deborah goes with him. She agrees but warns him that the glory will go to a woman. Sisera's army has 900 iron chariots. God confounds Sisera's army; Sisera flees on foot. He seeks refuge with Yael (יָעֵל), wife of Heber the Kenite. She gives him milk, covers him with a blanket. When he falls asleep, Yael drives a tent peg through his temple and kills him. Barak arrives and sees Sisera dead. Chapter 5 is the Song of Deborah — one of the oldest poems in the Bible, celebrating the victory. Famous line: "The stars fought from heaven, the stars in their courses fought against Sisera." The song ends with Sisera's mother waiting at the window for her son who will never return.
When & Why

Read as haftarah for Parashat Beshalach because Beshalach contains the Song of the Sea (Shirat HaYam) — both portions contain famous victory songs sung after a miraculous military triumph.

Key Details
  • Deborah = prophetess AND judge; sits under a palm tree
  • Barak won't go without Deborah → glory goes to a woman (Yael)
  • Sisera: 900 iron chariots; flees on foot after defeat
  • Yael kills Sisera with a tent peg through his temple
  • Song of Deborah = ancient victory poem (one of oldest in Tanakh)
  • "Stars fought against Sisera" — cosmic imagery
  • Ends poignantly with Sisera's mother waiting in vain
Micah 5:6–6:8 Parashat Balak
The Remnant of Jacob & "What Does God Require?"
The remnant of Jacob will be "like dew from God, like showers on the grass that do not wait for man or linger for mortals." Also like a lion among the nations. God will cut off Israel's horses, chariots, and sorceries. In chapter 6, God holds a court case (ריב) against Israel: "What have I done to you? How have I wearied you? I brought you out of Egypt, redeemed you from slavery, sent Moses, Aaron, and Miriam." God reminds them of Balak and Bilam — what Balak plotted and what Bilam answered. Someone asks: what shall I bring God — calves? Thousands of rams? Rivers of oil? Shall I give my firstborn for my sin? Micah's famous answer: "What does the LORD require of you but to do justice (מִשְׁפָּט), love kindness (חֶסֶד), and walk humbly with your God (הַצְנֵעַ לֶכֶת עִם אֱלֹהֶיךָ)."
When & Why

Read for Parashat Balak because Balak and Bilam appear both in the Torah portion and in this haftarah — God explicitly references the Balak episode as evidence of His faithfulness to Israel.

Key Details
  • Remnant of Jacob = like dew AND like a lion (both images)
  • God's court case: "What have I done to you?"
  • Balak and Bilam referenced explicitly in Micah 6
  • Famous triad: do justice + love kindness + walk humbly
  • This verse (6:8) is one of the most famous in all of Tanakh
Hosea 11:7–12:12 Parashat Vayishlach
God's Anguish Over Ephraim & Jacob's Legacy
God is anguished over Israel (called Ephraim): "My heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender. I will not execute my fierce anger… for I am God and not a man, the Holy One in your midst." Yet Ephraim has surrounded God with lies. Judah also wanders. God holds a case against Israel and will punish Jacob according to his ways. The passage then reviews Jacob's story: he grabbed his brother's heel in the womb; he strove with God (alluding to the wrestling); he wept and sought favor; at Beit El God found him and spoke with him. "So you must return to your God; keep justice and righteousness, and wait continually for your God."
When & Why

Read for Parashat Vayishlach, which contains Jacob's wrestling with the angel, his reunion with Esau, and his time at Beit El. The haftarah reviews Jacob's history through the prophetic lens of Hosea.

Key Details
  • "I am God and not a man" — reason God's compassion overcomes anger
  • Jacob reviewed: grabbed heel in womb + wrestled God + found at Beit El
  • Jacob wept and sought favor — at what encounter? (wrestling/angel)
  • Call to return: keep justice, wait for God
Ketuvim Eichah — Lamentations אֵיכָה · Read on Tisha B'Av
▾
Eichah 1 Acrostic — Alef to Tav
Jerusalem Sits Alone — The Desolate City
"Eichah (אֵיכָה) — How does the city sit solitary that was full of people!" Jerusalem, once great among nations, is now like a widow. She weeps bitterly in the night, tears on her cheeks. All who honored her despise her; she has seen her nakedness. Her enemies have become her masters; her children have gone into exile. This chapter personifies Jerusalem as a grieving woman (אָבֵל) who laments her own sin: "The LORD is in the right, for I have rebelled against His word." She cries out: "Is it nothing to you, all you who pass by? Look and see if there is any sorrow like my sorrow." 22 verses, each beginning with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet (acrostic).
Key Details
  • Each chapter is an acrostic (Aleph-Bet), except chapter 5
  • Jerusalem personified as a widow/grieving woman
  • "Is it nothing to you?" — call to passersby
  • Jerusalem acknowledges: "The LORD is right; I have rebelled"
  • Traditional author: Jeremiah (though anonymous in the text)
Eichah 2 Acrostic — God's Anger Described
God Covered Zion with Darkness
This chapter focuses on God as the agent of destruction — unusual and theologically bold. "The LORD has destroyed without mercy all the dwelling places of Jacob; He has thrown down the strongholds of Judah." God has "swallowed up" Israel; He has destroyed His own Temple like an enemy. The enemy has entered the sanctuary; prophets receive no vision. The poet tells Zion to "cry out to the Lord" — pour out your heart like water before the face of God. The chapter ends with the shocking image of mothers boiling their own children — a reference to the famine during the siege of Jerusalem.
Key Details
  • God is portrayed as the active agent of Zion's destruction
  • God "destroyed His own Temple like an enemy" — shocking idea
  • False prophets received no vision
  • Famine so severe: mothers boiling their own children
  • "Pour out your heart like water before God" — raw prayer imagery
Eichah 3 Triple Acrostic — Longest Chapter
The Man Who Has Seen Affliction — Hope in Darkness
The longest chapter — 66 verses, with each letter of the alphabet used for three consecutive verses. A single male narrator ("I am the man who has seen affliction") describes deep personal suffering: darkness, heavy chains, broken bones, mockery. Yet in the middle comes the turning point — the most hopeful verses: "This I recall to mind; therefore I have hope: The steadfast love (חַסְדֵי) of the LORD never ceases, His mercies never end. They are new every morning — great is Your faithfulness (אֱמוּנָתְךָ)." God is good to those who wait for Him. It is good to bear the yoke in youth. The chapter also calls for communal reflection: "Let us examine and probe our ways, and return to God."
Key Details
  • MOST FAMOUS chapter — center of the scroll; center of hope
  • "Chasidei Hashem lo tamnu" — God's steadfast love never ceases
  • "New every morning — great is Your faithfulness (emunah)"
  • Triple acrostic: each letter gets 3 verses (66 total)
  • "Let us examine our ways and return to God" — call to teshuvah
  • Narrator shifts from individual to communal voice
Eichah 4 Acrostic — The Siege
How the Gold Has Grown Dim
"How the gold has grown dim, how the pure gold has changed! The sacred stones lie scattered at the head of every street." This refers to the people — once like precious gold, now worthless. The children of Zion, once worth their weight in gold, are now regarded like clay pots. Even jackals give their young milk, but the daughter of Zion has become cruel to her children because of the siege's famine. The fate of the Nazirites, once pure and white, is described — now shriveled and black. The kings of the earth did not believe an enemy could enter Jerusalem's gates. It happened because of the sins of the priests and prophets who shed innocent blood. The chapter ends with a word to Edom: do not rejoice, for your cup of punishment will come too.
Key Details
  • "How the gold has grown dim" — people described as precious metals now tarnished
  • Even jackals nurse their young — contrast to mothers during siege
  • Nazirites' physical appearance destroyed by the famine
  • Sins of priests and prophets blamed for the destruction
  • Warning to Edom: your cup of punishment comes next
Eichah 5 NOT an Acrostic — Prayer/Plea
Remember, O LORD — The Final Prayer
The final chapter is a communal prayer (not an acrostic — the only exception). "Remember, O LORD, what has befallen us; look and see our disgrace." The nation describes its suffering: orphans, widows, strangers ruling over them, famine, being pursued. "The crown has fallen from our head. Woe to us that we have sinned." The chapter ends with the most famous verses of the book — chanted aloud before the final verse: "Return us to You, O LORD, and we will return; renew our days as of old (הֲשִׁיבֵנוּ אֱלֹהֵינוּ אֵלֶיךָ וְנָשׁוּבָה, חַדֵּשׁ יָמֵינוּ כְּקֶדֶם)." The final (troubling) verse — "unless You have utterly rejected us" — is repeated in synagogue after the second-to-last verse so the book does not end on despair.
Key Details
  • Only chapter in Eichah that is NOT an acrostic
  • "Hashivenu" (ch. 5:21) — sung aloud in synagogue before final verse
  • "The crown has fallen" — complete humiliation
  • Final verse repeated before last verse so book doesn't end in despair
  • This chapter = communal prayer / petition (different tone from rest)
